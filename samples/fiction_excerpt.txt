The harbor was quiet the morning Mara came home. She stood at the end of the pier in her father's old grey coat, the one with the torn pocket she had never bothered to mend, and watched the fishing boats slide out past the breakwater. Seven years away, and the town smelled exactly as she had left it: salt, diesel, and the faint rot of kelp drying on the rocks.

Her brother Jack was waiting by the harbourmaster's hut, taller than she remembered, a cigarette cupped against the wind. He did not wave. That was Jack — he would rather wait an hour in the cold than admit he was glad to see anyone. When she reached him he only nodded, took her bag, and said, "You're late. He's been asking for you since Tuesday."

"How is he," Mara said, though she already knew from the careful way Jack would not meet her eyes.

"Dying," Jack said. "Slowly. The doctor from Aberdeen comes twice a week now. Costs a fortune we don't have." He started up the hill toward the house without waiting for her, the way he had walked ahead of her their whole childhood, and Mara followed, the way she always had.

Their father, Tom Ferris, had been the steadiest man in the village for forty years — the one who mended other people's nets for free, who pulled two boys from the water in the storm of '89 and never once mentioned it. Now he lay in the back bedroom under three blankets, his big hands gone soft and useless on the quilt, and when he saw Mara in the doorway his whole ruined face opened like a window.

"There she is," he whispered. "There's my girl."

Mara had rehearsed a hundred cold things to say to him on the train north. She had practised them for seven years. Standing there, she could not remember a single one. She crossed the room and took his hand, and Jack stood in the hall behind her, watching, and for a moment the three of them were a family again, the way they had not been since her mother died.

Later, in the kitchen, Jack put the kettle on and they argued the way they always argued — quietly, fiercely, about money, about the house, about who had stayed and who had run. "You left," Jack said. "You don't get to come back and have opinions."

"I came back," she said.

"For how long? Until he's in the ground and the house is sold?"

She did not answer, because she did not know, and Jack saw that she did not know, and something in him went hard and quiet again. He set a mug of tea in front of her anyway. That was the thing about Jack: his anger and his kindness came out of the same place, and you could never have one without the other.

Old Bess, the neighbour who had looked in on Tom every day since the spring, let herself in without knocking, as she always did, carrying a covered dish. "Soup," she announced. "He won't eat it, but you two will." She looked Mara up and down with her sharp little eyes. "You've got your mother's chin. God help you." And then, more gently, putting a hand on Mara's arm: "He waited for you, love. He told everyone you'd come. Don't let him be wrong about that."

That night Mara sat by her father's bed while he slept, listening to the tide come in, and understood that she was not going to leave again, not for a long time, maybe not ever, and that this was either the bravest thing she had ever done or the most cowardly, and that she might never know which.
